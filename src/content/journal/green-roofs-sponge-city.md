Cities seal surfaces — and rain that once soaked into the ground now races across asphalt into the sewer. During heavy rain, that sewer is quickly overwhelmed. Green roofs reverse the effect: they hold water where it falls.

## The roof as a buffer

Depending on the build-up, an extensively greened roof stores a substantial share of annual rainfall and releases it with delay — through evaporation and slow run-off. For the sewer system this means the peak is capped: not all the water arrives at once, but dampened and time-shifted.

This is exactly the idea of the **sponge city**: surfaces should absorb and hold water like a sponge instead of shedding it immediately. A green roof is one of the few components that does this on otherwise unused space.

## Cooling through evaporation

Every litre of water that evaporates through the plants draws heat from its surroundings. Greened roofs heat up far less in summer than bituminous surfaces — lowering the temperature beneath the roof and softening the urban heat-island effect. The build-up also insulates year-round.

## Biodiversity is not accidental

A species-rich roof is not made by a single sedum mat. What matters is substrate depth and structure, a mix of native, drought-tolerant species, and deliberate features — small sand and gravel lenses, deadwood, stone piles. This is how wild bees, beetles and birds find food and nesting sites.

## What matters in the build-up

- **Structure first:** substrate and saturated water have weight; load capacity decides the possible build-up.
- **Substrate, not topsoil:** special mineral substrates store water and stay structurally stable.
- **Species by site:** sun, wind and substrate depth decide what grows — not the catalogue.
- **Plan for care:** the first years decide whether the intended vegetation establishes.

A well-planned green roof is more than looks: measurable water retention, tangible cooling and real habitat — on a surface that would otherwise lie idle.
