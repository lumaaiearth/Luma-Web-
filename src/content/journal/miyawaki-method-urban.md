A forest on the footprint of a few parking spaces? The method named after the Japanese botanist Akira Miyawaki makes exactly that possible — delivering in a few years what conventional plantings take decades to achieve.

## The principle: density over spacing

Conventional plantings space trees widely. Miyawaki inverts this: **three to five saplings per square metre**, densely mixed, in several layers — trees, shrubs, ground cover. The plants compete for light and therefore grow upward faster. The result is not a flower bed but a young forest ecosystem.

## The soil decides

The real core of the method lies below the surface. Before planting, the often-compacted urban soil is loosened deeply and enriched with organic material and soil life. A healthy soil microbiome is the engine: it feeds the roots, stores water and makes the planting resilient.

## Native species only

The planting uses what naturally belongs at the site — the potential natural vegetation. Native species are adapted to climate and soil, need less care, and offer real habitat to native insects and birds. Exotics may look similar but do little ecologically.

## Why it fits the city

- **Small sites:** courtyards, leftover strips and street edges are enough.
- **Fast effect:** within a few years the dense canopy cools noticeably.
- **Low care after establishment:** after about three years the micro-forest is largely left to itself.
- **Biodiversity in tight space:** many species, many layers, much life.

The first three years are decisive: watering, gap-filling, refreshing the microbiome. After that the small forest sustains itself — a living air conditioner in the middle of the city.
