A flower strip looks good and provides pollen. But many of the 500-plus wild-bee species in Germany still miss out — because they lack something else: a place to nest.

## Food and nesting belong together

Most wild bees are solitary. They build no colonies but lay individual nests — in bare ground, in deadwood, in pithy stems or in steep walls of sand and loam. Without these structures, even the finest flower strip helps little: the bees find food, but no nesting site within reach. As a rule of thumb, food and nest should be close together — often only a few hundred metres apart.

## Specialists need the right plants

Many species are specialised on particular plant families (oligolectic). They collect pollen from just a few genera. An offering of mostly double-flowered ornamentals or exotic species passes them by. What counts are **native wild plants** — and a continuous sequence of bloom from early spring (willows, crocus) into late summer.

## What actually helps

- **Bare ground:** unsealed, sunny sand or loam patches for ground-nesting species.
- **Deadwood and pithy stems:** left standing and lying — not "tidied up".
- **Sand and steep walls:** deliberately built structures for specialised species.
- **Native bloom sequence:** continuous from March to September, not one short peak.
- **No insecticide, little mowing:** and if you mow, do it in sections.

A ready-made "insect hotel" from the hardware store is, at best, a start. Effective wild-bee support plans food, nesting site and care together — as habitat, not decoration.
